What is Carbon Footprint?

A carbon footprint is a measure of the total amount of greenhouse gases, specifically carbon dioxide (CO2) and other carbon compounds, that are emitted directly or indirectly by an individual, organization, event, or product throughout its lifecycle. Greenhouse gases trap heat in the Earth's atmosphere, contributing to the phenomenon known as global warming and climate change. The carbon footprint concept helps quantify the impact of human activities on the environment in terms of their contribution to the accumulation of greenhouse gases. It is usually measured in units of carbon dioxide equivalents (CO2e), which express the total impact of various greenhouse gases in terms of the amount of CO2 that would have the equivalent warming effect.

Carbon footprints can be categorized into two main types: Direct Emissions (Scope 1): These emissions are produced directly by an individual, organization, or product. Examples include the combustion of fossil fuels for heating, transportation, and industrial processes. Indirect Emissions (Scopes 2 and 3): Scope 2 Emissions: These are indirect emissions associated with the production of the electricity, heat, or steam purchased and used by an entity. Scope 3 Emissions: These are indirect emissions that occur in the value chain of the entity, including activities such as supply chain, transportation, and product use. This is often the largest portion of an entity's carbon footprint and can be challenging to measure and control.
